# Analysis Phase

## DEC Learning Hub Course Creator Portal Core Workflow Specification

## A. What the Analysis Phase Is

The **Analysis Phase** is the evidence and baseline foundation of the DEC Learning Hub Course Creator Portal. It is the phase where real CSO capacity problems are translated into structured platform data before any course is designed, built, reviewed, or published.

In the DEC model, course creation must not begin from a blank page or from a general request such as “create a training on governance” or “make a course on fundraising.” Instead, every course must begin from a validated capacity need, supported by participatory evidence, baseline data, root-cause diagnosis, and clear linkage to CSO performance outcomes.

The deeper analysis itself may happen partly outside the platform. This can include participatory scoping, interviews, focus group discussions, CSO self-assessments, donor feedback, community consultations, organizational reviews, and field-based diagnosis. The platform does not need to reproduce all of this fieldwork as software features. Its role is to capture the approved outputs of that process as structured inputs that guide the course creation workflow.

The Analysis Phase therefore converts field reality into platform-ready evidence, including:

- the priority CSO capacity gap;
- the affected learner group;
- the current baseline or current practice;
- the desired capacity or performance standard;
- the evidence source;
- the root cause of the gap;
- the relevant CSO capacity area;
- the relevant indicator, standard, or framework;
- the decision on whether the gap can be addressed through digital learning;
- the approved handover into the Design Phase.

This reflects the principle already established in the DEC materials: the Learning Hub should solve documented organizational vulnerabilities, not produce generic training content. The CSO Learning Design Handbook states that DEC courses should be based on empirical CSO capacity gaps and should use standards such as EU DG NEAR, OECD DAC, and the Global Standard for CSO Accountability to validate the diagnosis.

## B. Why the Analysis Phase Is Needed

The Analysis Phase is needed because the DEC Learning Hub is not a general-purpose e-learning platform. It is a capacity-strengthening system for local and grassroots CSOs. Its purpose is to help CSOs improve real organizational practice, mission delivery, accountability, sustainability, advocacy, safeguarding, MEAL, resource mobilization, and civic engagement.

Without a strong Analysis Phase, the platform risks producing courses that are attractive but irrelevant. A creator may build a polished course on “proposal writing” when the real problem is lack of community evidence. Another creator may build a course on “financial reporting” when the real barrier is lack of internet access, lack of data stipends, or an unrealistic donor reporting template. The Analysis Phase prevents this by requiring diagnosis before design.

The phase is needed for five reasons.

First, it **prevents generic course creation**. Course creators should not invent topics based on personal preference, donor fashion, or abstract training requests. They should build only from approved capacity gaps and strategic priorities.

Second, it **protects CSO time and attention**. Grassroots CSO staff operate under serious time, funding, safety, bandwidth, and compliance pressures. The platform should not ask them to complete courses that do not solve practical problems.

Third, it **links learning to mission performance**. Every course should help CSOs do something better: govern more transparently, mobilize resources, engage communities, monitor outcomes, advocate safely, comply with donor requirements, protect staff and communities, or build stronger partnerships.

Fourth, it **connects DEC learning to credible standards and indicators**. The DG NEAR framework, for example, organizes civil society strengthening around enabling environment, CSO-public authority cooperation, and CSO capacity and resilience, and includes specific objectives such as governance, transparency, MEAL, evidence use, partnerships, diversified funding, and human resources.

Fifth, it **creates the baseline for evaluation**. The Analysis Phase should define not only what course to build, but also what evidence will later show that the learning has contributed to improved practice. This aligns with the Handbook’s emphasis on moving beyond “smile sheets” toward action commitments, telemetry, and evidence of real organizational application.

## C. How the Analysis Phase Works: Step-by-Step Implementation Guide

### Step 1: Conduct participatory scoping and evidence intake

The process begins by identifying the real capacity challenges facing CSOs and the communities they serve. This should be informed by diverse voices, not only senior CSO leadership. The 7-Stage Capacity Transformation Cycle emphasizes that capacity priorities should be co-designed with grassroots CSOs and rights-holders, and that stakeholder mapping should include rights-holders, CSO field staff, CSO leadership, duty-bearers, and donors.

In practice, DEC staff, consortium advisors, CSO representatives, or capacity-building facilitators gather evidence through consultations, FGDs, interviews, organizational assessments, donor reports, community feedback, and existing CSO capacity data.

The platform captures the structured evidence intake, including the source of the finding, the affected CSO group, the practical problem observed, and why the problem matters for CSO performance or community outcomes.

### Step 2: Filter the issue through systems triage

Not every problem should become a course. The Analysis Phase must distinguish between a course-addressable capacity gap and a wider systemic barrier.

For example, if CSOs cannot advocate because of a restrictive legal environment, a course alone may not solve the problem. That issue may need legal support, coalition advocacy, policy engagement, or protection support. But if CSOs are struggling because they do not know how to prepare evidence-based policy briefs, that may be a valid learning intervention.

The 7-Stage document calls this the “Is this trainable?” check and recommends routing findings either to DEC Hub capacity interventions or to broader systemic interventions such as legal, advocacy, or coalition support.

The platform should therefore include a simple routing decision:

- **Course-addressable gap**: move forward into capacity diagnosis.
- **Partly course-addressable gap**: combine digital learning with mentoring, coaching, tools, or peer learning.
- **Systemic/environmental gap**: route to non-learning support and do not proceed directly to course design.

### Step 3: Link the issue to a CSO capacity area

Once a problem is judged potentially course-addressable, it must be linked to one or more DEC CSO capacity areas. These should form the core taxonomy of the Analysis Phase.

Recommended final capacity areas:

1.  Internal Governance and Leadership

2.  Transparency and Accountability

3.  Strategic Planning and Organizational Sustainability

4.  Financial Management and Resource Mobilization

5.  Human Resources, Inclusion, and Safeguarding

6.  Evidence-Based Advocacy and Civic Engagement

7.  Monitoring, Evaluation, Accountability, and Learning

8.  Digital Skills and Data Use

9.  Networking, Partnerships, and Collective Action

These areas should appear in the platform as controlled dropdowns or linked taxonomy fields. They should not be open-ended text fields only. This allows DEC to track which capacity areas are most frequently diagnosed, which courses respond to them, and which indicators later show improvement.

### Step 4: Map the capacity area to standards, indicators, and baseline

The selected capacity gap should be mapped to a relevant indicator, standard, or framework. This may include EU DG NEAR indicators, the Global Standard for CSO Accountability, OECD DAC guidance, HRBA principles, safeguarding standards, DEC’s internal framework, or locally adapted CSO capacity indicators.

This mapping helps ensure that the course is not only useful locally, but also produces credible and transportable evidence for reporting, donor dialogue, peer learning, and adaptive capacity support.

The platform should capture:

- capacity area;
- related standard or indicator;
- current baseline status;
- desired capacity status;
- evidence source;
- means of verification;
- expected change.

For example, a course on financial sustainability should not simply be tagged as “fundraising.” It should be linked to a more precise capacity objective such as diversified funding base, local resource mobilization, private-sector partnership readiness, budget transparency, or donor compliance.

The DG NEAR framework provides useful examples of this type of indicator anchoring, including CSO governance, public communication, funding transparency, MEAL, evidence use, partnerships, diversified funding, and effective human resources.

### Step 5: Apply the three CSO needs-analysis questions

The platform should guide the creator or capacity advisor through three adapted needs-analysis questions:

1.  **What are CSOs currently doing?
    **This defines the current practice, baseline, or observed performance gap.

2.  **What do CSOs need to do to achieve their mission and capacity objectives?
    **This defines the desired practice, standard, or performance outcome.

3.  **Why are CSOs not doing this now?
    **This identifies the root cause behind the gap.

This prevents the platform from treating symptoms as course topics. Analysis 1 already captures this well by adapting the three needs-analysis questions to current reality, desired organizational performance, and root cause.

The output of this step should be a clear **organizational practice gap statement**, not a course title.

Example:

> “CSO field staff are collecting attendance data but are not using outcome-level evidence to show whether community behavior, access, or accountability has changed.”

This is stronger than:

> “Create a course on MEAL.”

### Step 6: Diagnose the root cause using K/S/M/E

The platform should require the gap to be diagnosed using the four diagnostic buckets:

| **Diagnostic bucket** | **Meaning**                                                                                      | **Learning implication**                                                          |
|-----------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Knowledge             | People lack facts, rules, definitions, or updated information                                    | Job aid, checklist, short explainer, reference guide                              |
| Skill                 | People know the concept but cannot perform the task well                                         | Practice, scenario, simulation, worked example, guided exercise                   |
| Motivation            | People can do it but lack confidence, incentives, trust, or buy-in                               | Peer story, reflection, leadership message, case example, accountability dialogue |
| Environment           | People are blocked by tools, policy, funding, time, safety, infrastructure, or legal constraints | Route to non-learning intervention or blended support                             |

The K/S/M/E diagnosis is critical because it determines whether a digital course is appropriate and what type of learning design should follow. The DEC sources repeatedly emphasize that the platform should use this diagnostic gate to avoid building courses for problems that are actually environmental or systemic.

The final version should avoid overly rigid “abort” language. Instead, it should use **intervention routing**. Some environment gaps may fully block course creation, while others may require a blended intervention: learning plus tools, mentoring, templates, peer exchange, micro-grants, or policy support.

### Step 7: Define the intervention routing decision

After the K/S/M/E diagnosis, the platform should produce an intervention routing decision.

Recommended routing logic:

- **Knowledge gap** → short course, job aid, checklist, explainer, reference card.
- **Skill gap** → scenario-based course, practice task, simulation, guided exercise.
- **Motivation gap** → peer-learning case, reflection, leadership framing, accountability story, facilitated discussion.
- **Environment gap** → route to non-learning support, advocacy, systems improvement, funding support, policy engagement, infrastructure support, or blended intervention.
- **Mixed gap** → design a combined package, such as short learning + template + peer review + action commitment.

This routing decision should be visible in the portal before the Design Phase begins. It should shape the recommended course type, block types, AI authoring guardrails, review checklist, and evaluation method.

### Step 8: Validate and approve the analysis record

Before the course creator proceeds to Design, the analysis record should be reviewed and approved by an authorized DEC reviewer, capacity advisor, or consortium admin.

The review should check:

- Is the problem evidence-based?
- Has it been validated by relevant CSO actors or community stakeholders?
- Is the target learner group clear?
- Is the root cause plausible?
- Is this a learning-addressable issue?
- Is the baseline defined?
- Is the indicator or standard mapped?
- Is the intervention routing decision appropriate?
- Is the issue safe and ethical to address through a digital course?

Once approved, the analysis record becomes locked as the official evidence base for the course.

### Step 9: Generate the Analysis-to-Design Handover

The final output of the Analysis Phase is the **Analysis-to-Design Handover**. This is a concise, structured record that carries the evidence base into the Design Phase.

It should include:

- approved capacity gap;
- learner group;
- baseline status;
- desired performance;
- standard or indicator linkage;
- root cause diagnosis;
- intervention routing decision;
- key learner constraints;
- evaluation indicator or action commitment;
- reviewer approval status.

Analysis 2 already provides a strong basis for this idea by describing a handover manifest that locks the objective and baseline, then unlocks the Design Phase after admin approval.

## D. Platform Design Implications

The Analysis Phase should be designed in the platform as a **structured data workspace**, not as a long text document upload area.

The platform should include the following design implications.

### 1. Master Capacity Gap Bank

The platform should maintain an approved list of capacity gaps derived from participatory scoping, diagnosis, and DEC strategic priorities. Course creators select from this bank rather than starting from unsupported course ideas.

This aligns with the 7-Stage model, where participatory scoping produces a validated Master Topic Bank that governs what creators are allowed to build for a defined period.

### 2. Analysis Dashboard

The dashboard should show approved capacity areas, diagnosed gaps, baseline status, priority level, evidence sources, target groups, and readiness for course design.

### 3. Controlled Taxonomy and Dropdowns

The platform should use structured dropdowns for:

- CSO capacity area;
- target learner group;
- evidence source;
- standard or indicator;
- K/S/M/E diagnosis;
- intervention route;
- baseline status;
- approval status.

This reduces inconsistency and supports later reporting.

### 4. Evidence and Baseline Fields

Each analysis record should include required baseline and evidence fields. These fields should later feed into the Evaluate phase.

### 5. Diagnostic Gate

The platform should include a simple diagnostic gate that checks whether the issue is suitable for digital learning, blended learning, or non-learning support.

### 6. Persistent Reference Panel

Once approved, the analysis summary should remain visible in the Design and Build phases. This prevents course drift and keeps creators focused on the original capacity gap, learner reality, baseline, and target outcome.

### 7. AI Authoring Guardrails

The AI authoring assistant should not generate course content from an empty prompt. It should use the approved analysis record as context: capacity area, learner group, standard, baseline, root cause, desired practice, and intervention type.

### 8. Approval and Locking

After admin approval, core analysis fields should be locked. Any change to the capacity gap, indicator, baseline, or root cause should trigger a review update.

### 9. Evaluation Linkage

The Analysis Phase should generate the first version of the evaluation logic. This may include baseline, intended outcome, action commitment, means of verification, and expected data to collect after course delivery.

## E. Final Output of the Analysis Phase

By the end of the Analysis Phase, the platform should produce an approved **Analysis-to-Design Handover Record**.

This record should be short, structured, and usable by designers, reviewers, AI tools, and evaluators.

Minimum content:

1.  **Capacity Gap Title
    **A concise title for the approved capacity gap.

2.  **Capacity Area
    **One of the DEC CSO capacity areas.

3.  **Target Learner Group
    **The specific CSO role or group affected.

4.  **Evidence Source
    **Consultation, assessment, donor report, community feedback, CSO self-assessment, review finding, or other evidence.

5.  **Current Baseline
    **What CSOs are currently doing or not doing.

6.  **Desired Practice / Capacity Objective
    **What CSOs should be able to do.

7.  **Standard / Indicator Linkage
    **Relevant EU, OECD, Global Standard, HRBA, safeguarding, or DEC indicator.

8.  **Root Cause Diagnosis
    **Knowledge, Skill, Motivation, Environment, or mixed diagnosis.

9.  **Intervention Routing Decision
    **Course, job aid, scenario, peer learning, mentoring, blended support, or non-learning referral.

10. **Learner Constraints
    **Language, literacy, bandwidth, device, safety, workload, context, or accessibility issues.

11. **Evaluation Anchor
    **Baseline, action commitment, means of verification, or expected post-course evidence.

12. **Approval Status
    **Draft, pending review, approved, returned for revision, or routed to non-learning support.

Once this output is approved, the Design Phase begins.

# Tools and Templates

To keep the system practical and not overloaded, the Analysis Phase should use only **five core tools/templates**:

1.  **Participatory Scoping and Evidence Intake Template
    **Captures stakeholder input, evidence source, affected CSO group, and the observed capacity problem.

2.  **CSO Capacity Area and Indicator Mapping Matrix
    **Links the problem to DEC capacity areas, relevant standards, indicators, baseline status, and desired capacity objective.

3.  **Needs Analysis and Root-Cause Diagnosis Worksheet
    **Uses the three needs-analysis questions and 5 Whys to define the actual organizational practice gap.

4.  **K/S/M/E Intervention Routing Matrix
    **Categorizes the root cause and recommends the appropriate intervention route: job aid, scenario, peer learning, blended support, or non-learning referral.

5.  **Analysis-to-Design Handover Record
    **The final approved platform output that locks the evidence base and transfers the course into the Design Phase.

### Tool 2: CSO Capacity Area and Indicator Mapping Matrix

- a DEC CSO capacity area;
- a specific capacity objective;
- a relevant standard, framework, or indicator;
- the current baseline;
- the desired capacity level;
- the evidence needed to verify improvement;
- the platform decision on whether the issue should proceed to deeper diagnosis and course design.

This tool ensures that DEC Learning Hub courses are not built from broad topics such as “fundraising,” “governance,” or “MEAL,” but from specific, evidence-based capacity gaps that can be tracked, designed for, reviewed, and evaluated.

The tool draws strongly from DG NEAR’s civil society capacity indicators, which include governance, transparency, MEAL, evidence use, partnerships, diversified funding, and human resources. It also reflects the Global Standard’s emphasis on benchmarks, baselines, stakeholder feedback, self-evaluation, peer review, and targeted capacity support.

### Tool 3: Needs Analysis and Root-Cause Diagnosis Worksheet

1\. Purpose of the Tool

This tool is used after the **CSO Capacity Area and Indicator Mapping Matrix**. Its purpose is to move from a validated and indicator-linked capacity gap to a clear diagnosis of **why the gap exists** and what kind of intervention is appropriate.

In the DEC Learning Hub, this tool protects the platform from becoming a generic course factory. It helps course creators, DEC capacity advisors, and reviewers avoid the common mistake of turning every problem into a course. - passive content-only lessons;
- quizzes as the main proof of learning;
- abstract definitions without practice.

**Skill route decision:**

8.3 If the route is Motivation / Mindset

Use relevance, confidence, trust, and ownership strategies.

Recommended design:

- peer story from a comparable CSO;
- “why this matters” reflection;
- leadership or community voice;
- social proof;
- values-based prompt;
- action commitment;
- facilitated discussion where appropriate.

Avoid:

- blaming learners;
- generic motivational language;
- donor-compliance-only framing;
- presenting the issue as individual weakness when the barrier is structural.

**Motivation route decision:**

8.4 If the route is Environment / Resource

Do not force a course-only solution.

Recommended response:

- route to advocacy or policy support;
- route to funding or infrastructure support;
- route to safeguarding or protection support;
- route to organizational systems strengthening;
- provide tools or templates where useful;
- combine learning with mentoring only if learning can realistically help.

Avoid:

- building a course that asks CSOs to do what they lack the resources, authority, safety, or legal space to do.

**Environment route decision:**

8.5 If the route is Mixed

Design a blended package.

Recommended response:

- identify the dominant barrier;
- design a small learning product for the learning-addressable part;
- add tools, templates, mentoring, or peer support;
- route non-learning barriers to the appropriate support pathway;
- define what the course can and cannot solve.

Avoid:

- hiding systemic barriers under a generic course;
- overloading the course with too many objectives.

**Mixed route decision:**

### Tool 5: Analysis-to-Design Handover Record

1\. Purpose of the Tool

This tool is the final output of the **Analysis Phase**. Its purpose is to consolidate the approved results from the previous four tools into one clear, structured handover record that the **Design Phase** can use.

The handover record is not a full course outline. It is the approved evidence base that tells the designer, course creator, reviewer, and AI authoring assistant:

- what capacity gap the course or intervention is responding to;
- who the target learners are;
- what evidence supports the need;
- what baseline exists;
- what desired practice is expected;
- what indicator or standard the intervention is linked to;
- what root cause has been diagnosed;
- what intervention route has been approved;
- what the course should and should not try to solve;
- what safeguards, constraints, and evaluation anchors must be carried forward.

Once this record is approved, the platform should lock the core analysis fields and open the appropriate Design Phase workflow.
