# Design Phase

## DEC Learning Hub Course Creator Portal

## Core Workflow Specification

## A. What the Design Phase Is

The Design Phase is the structured bridge between the approved Analysis Phase and the actual building of a course. It is not yet content writing, video production, AI authoring, or block-based course assembly. It is the phase where the approved evidence from analysis is converted into a clear, practical, and reviewable learning design.

The Design Phase begins only after the Analysis-to-Design Handover Record has been approved. This handover provides the locked evidence base: the capacity gap, target learner group, baseline, indicator or standard, root-cause diagnosis, K/S/M/E intervention route, learner constraints, safeguards, and evaluation anchor.

The purpose of the Design Phase is to transform that evidence into:

- a practical performance goal;
- a clear action map;
- a course blueprint;
- a storyboard or block plan;
- suitable practice activities;
- assessment and action commitment logic;
- a Design-to-Build Handover Record.

In simple terms, the Analysis Phase answers:

What capacity problem are we solving, for whom, and why?

The Design Phase answers:

What must learners actually do differently, how will they practice it, and how will we know the course is ready to build?

This phase ensures that no course moves into Build as a generic topic such as “fundraising,” “MEAL,” or “governance.” Instead, every course must be designed around a specific, evidence-based CSO performance need.

## B. Why the Design Phase Is Needed

The Design Phase is needed because many capacity-building courses fail at the exact point where diagnosis turns into content. A real capacity problem is identified, but the course creator immediately starts writing explanations, slides, or modules. This produces information-heavy training that may look complete but does not change practice.

The Design Phase prevents this by requiring creators to design backwards from practical action.

It ensures that each course is:

- grounded in the approved Analysis Phase;
- linked to a real CSO capacity gap and baseline;
- focused on observable practice, not only knowledge transfer;
- aligned with the correct K/S/M/E intervention route;
- realistic for local and grassroots CSO working conditions;
- safe for sensitive civic space, safeguarding, accountability, and advocacy contexts;
- appropriate for mobile-first and low-bandwidth delivery;
- ready for AI-assisted authoring without drifting into generic content;
- connected to evaluation evidence from the beginning.

This phase protects CSO learners’ time. It also protects DEC’s quality standards by making sure that course creators do not jump from “CSOs need capacity building” to a long theory course. Instead, they must show what the learner will practice, what output they will produce, and how that output relates to the original capacity gap.

The Design Phase is therefore a design-control layer. It is lean enough to be practical, but rigorous enough to protect relevance, quality, safety, and traceability.

## C. Who Does What in the Design Phase

| Actor | Role in the Design Phase |
| --- | --- |
| Course creator / subject matter expert | Drafts the action map, course blueprint, storyboard, and practice activities based on the approved analysis record. |
| DEC capacity lead | Checks that the design remains aligned with the CSO capacity objective, baseline, and intended organizational change. |
| Instructional design reviewer | Checks action mapping, sequencing, learner workload, practice quality, assessment logic, and clarity. |
| Safeguarding / civic-space reviewer | Reviews sensitive topics, scenarios, data protection issues, advocacy risks, and do-no-harm concerns. |
| Platform | Pulls approved Analysis data, displays it as a read-only reference, validates required design fields, and controls whether Build can open. |
| AI assistant | Supports drafting only within the approved design context; it should not invent new course objectives, target groups, scenarios, or assessment logic. |

## D. Step-by-Step Design Workflow

## Step 1: Review the approved Analysis-to-Design Handover Record

The creator begins by reviewing the approved Analysis-to-Design Handover Record. The platform should display this record as a read-only reference panel throughout the Design Phase.

This panel should include:

- capacity gap;
- target learner group;
- baseline;
- desired capacity objective;
- relevant indicator or standard;
- root-cause diagnosis;
- K/S/M/E intervention route;
- learner constraints;
- safeguards;
- evaluation anchor.

The creator should not be able to change these core fields during Design without sending the record back for review.

## Step 2: Confirm the capacity objective and target learner group

The creator confirms who the course is for and what capacity objective it will support.

The learner group should be specific. For example:

- CSO board members;
- project officers;
- finance officers;
- MEAL officers;
- safeguarding focal persons;
- advocacy staff;
- field coordinators;
- volunteer leaders;
- local CSO executive directors.

The capacity objective should describe the improvement expected in CSO practice. It should not be vague.

Weak objective:

Learners will understand accountability.

Stronger objective:

CSO field staff will be able to use a simple community feedback log to record, categorize, and escalate community complaints safely.

## Step 3: Convert the capacity gap into a performance goal

The creator translates the capacity gap into a practical performance goal. This goal should describe what the learner or organization should be able to do better after the course or learning intervention.

The goal should be:

- specific;
- realistic;
- observable;
- linked to the approved capacity area;
- connected to the baseline and evaluation anchor.

Examples:

| Capacity area | Weak topic | Strong performance goal |
| --- | --- | --- |
| MEAL | Understand outcome monitoring | Use one simple outcome indicator to analyze whether an activity contributed to change. |
| Financial management | Learn donor budgeting | Prepare a basic donor budget using allowable cost categories and clear justification. |
| Advocacy | Learn advocacy | Draft a short evidence-based policy message using community data and safe language. |
| Governance | Board leadership | Identify and manage a conflict-of-interest situation using the organization’s governance procedure. |
| Safeguarding | Safeguarding awareness | Apply the correct referral pathway when a safeguarding concern is reported. |

## Step 4: Develop the Capacity Action Map

The creator then develops a Capacity Action Map. This is the core design method of the phase.

The action map should include four layers:

| Layer | Design question |
| --- | --- |
| Capacity goal | What practical organizational or performance goal must this course support? |
| Required actions | What must the learner actually do to achieve the goal? |
| Practice activities | How will the learner practice those actions in the course or blended activity? |
| Minimum information | What is the smallest amount of information needed to perform the action well? |

This structure prevents information dumping. Content is added only if it supports a required action.

Example:

| Action map layer | Example |
| --- | --- |
| Capacity goal | CSOs use community feedback to adapt project decisions. |
| Required action | Categorize feedback into complaint, suggestion, protection concern, or project quality issue. |
| Practice activity | Learner reviews five realistic community feedback examples and chooses the correct category and escalation pathway. |
| Minimum information | One-page guide explaining feedback categories and referral rules. |

The platform should flag objectives that use vague verbs such as “understand,” “be aware of,” or “learn about.” Creators should use action verbs such as:

- identify;
- draft;
- categorize;
- prioritize;
- calculate;
- apply;
- facilitate;
- document;
- escalate;
- analyze;
- revise;
- submit;
- compare;
- decide.

## Step 5: Identify essential knowledge only after actions are defined

After the action map is complete, the creator identifies the minimum information learners need to complete the practice activities.

This is a critical design rule:

Do not start with content. Start with action, then add only the knowledge needed to support action.

For example, if the performance goal is to help finance officers prepare a simple donor budget, learners may need a short explanation of allowable and unallowable costs. They do not need a long history of donor funding architecture.

The platform should encourage creators to remove:

- long theoretical introductions;
- donor jargon that does not support action;
- broad background sections;
- unnecessary policy history;
- repeated definitions;
- content that is not used in a practice task.

## Step 6: Select the learning pathway based on the K/S/M/E intervention route

The learning pathway should follow the approved root-cause diagnosis from the Analysis Phase.

| Diagnosis | Design pathway |
| --- | --- |
| Knowledge | Micro-learning, checklist, short explainer, job aid, quick knowledge check |
| Skill | Worked example, guided practice, simulation, scenario, practical assignment |
| Motivation / mindset | Peer story, reflection, leadership framing, community voice, action commitment |
| Environment / resource | Do not force a course-only solution; use referral, tool support, mentoring, advocacy, funding, or blended support |
| Mixed | Blended design combining short learning, tools, peer support, mentoring, and action follow-up |

A multiple-choice quiz may be useful for a knowledge gap. But for skill, judgment, safeguarding, advocacy, accountability, or applied MEAL gaps, the design should include practical tasks, scenarios, action commitments, or reviewed outputs.

## Step 7: Develop the Course Blueprint / Learning Design Document

The creator now turns the action map into a short course blueprint. This blueprint should be structured enough to guide Build, but not overloaded.

The blueprint should include:

- course title;
- target learner group;
- capacity area;
- capacity objective;
- performance goal;
- K/S/M/E route;
- course type;
- module or lesson sequence;
- practice activities;
- essential knowledge;
- assessment or action commitment;
- accessibility and bandwidth constraints;
- language or localization needs;
- safeguarding or civic-space constraints;
- evaluation anchor.

The blueprint should answer:

What is the course trying to change, what will learners practice, and what evidence will show readiness or application?

## Step 8: Create the Storyboard and Block Plan

The storyboard translates the blueprint into a block-by-block plan for the Build Phase.

The storyboard should not yet produce final polished content. It should define the intended learner experience.

For each lesson or block, the storyboard should specify:

- lesson title;
- block type;
- purpose of the block;
- key message or prompt;
- learner action;
- interaction type;
- media needs;
- accessibility requirements;
- language/localization notes;
- link to the relevant action map item;
- assessment or practice connection.

Recommended block types may include:

- short explainer;
- checklist;
- reflection prompt;
- scenario decision point;
- worked example;
- practical task;
- quiz or knowledge check;
- downloadable template;
- peer discussion prompt;
- action commitment;
- mentor or reviewer feedback task.

For low-bandwidth users, the storyboard should prioritize text, compressed graphics, audio where useful, downloadable templates, and mobile-friendly interactions. Heavy video should not be the default.

## Step 9: Plan scenarios and practice activities where needed

When the diagnosis involves skill, judgment, motivation, safeguarding, advocacy, accountability, or complex decision-making, the Design Phase should include a scenario or practice planner.

The recommended scenario structure is:

| Element | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Challenge | The realistic CSO situation or dilemma |
| Choices | Plausible options the learner may choose |
| Consequences | Realistic outcomes of each choice |
| Reflection | What the learner should notice or apply |
| Action link | How this connects to their real CSO work |

Scenarios should avoid obviously wrong choices. The options should reflect real pressures faced by local CSOs, such as donor compliance demands, community expectations, local authority pressure, limited internet, staff workload, safeguarding concerns, or data protection risks.

Example:

A woreda official asks a CSO field officer to share a beneficiary list that includes sensitive personal details. The learner must choose how to respond while protecting community members, maintaining a working relationship, and following data protection procedures.

The scenario should show consequences, not only “correct” or “incorrect” feedback.

## Step 10: Define assessment, action commitment, and evaluation linkage

The Design Phase must define how learning will be checked and how later application may be verified.

Assessment should match the root cause:

| Root cause | Suitable assessment |
| --- | --- |
| Knowledge | Short quiz, matching activity, checklist confirmation |
| Skill | Completed template, scenario decision, practical assignment |
| Motivation | Reflection, action commitment, peer discussion |
| Environment | Referral tracking, support plan, blended intervention record |
| Mixed | Combination of practical task, reflection, and follow-up evidence |

The preferred DEC approach should include an action commitment where appropriate. This means the learner identifies one practical step they will take in their organization after the course.

Examples:

- revise one section of a safeguarding referral pathway;
- draft one evidence-based advocacy message;
- complete a simple donor budget worksheet;
- categorize five community feedback entries;
- prepare one outcome evidence statement;
- update a board meeting conflict-of-interest checklist.

The design should also preserve the evaluation anchor from the Analysis Phase so the Evaluate phase can compare learning outputs and follow-up evidence against the original baseline.

## Step 11: Complete the Design Quality Gate

Before the Build Phase opens, the design should pass a quality review.

The reviewer should check:

| Quality question | Yes/No |
| --- | --- |
| Does the design use the approved Analysis-to-Design Handover Record? |   |
| Is the performance goal clear and observable? |   |
| Are the required actions practical and realistic? |   |
| Is essential knowledge limited to what supports action? |   |
| Is the learning pathway aligned with the K/S/M/E route? |   |
| Are scenarios and activities realistic for local CSOs? |   |
| Are safeguarding, civic-space, and data risks addressed? |   |
| Are low-bandwidth, mobile-first, accessibility, and language needs considered? |   |
| Is learner workload realistic? |   |
| Is there a practical assessment, output, or action commitment? |   |
| Is the evaluation anchor preserved? |   |
| Is the design ready for AI-assisted authoring and block-based Build? |   |

If the answer to major questions is “No,” the design should return for revision before Build is unlocked.

## Step 12: Prepare the Design-to-Build Handover

The final output of the Design Phase is the Design-to-Build Handover Record.

This handover should consolidate:

- approved analysis reference;
- capacity objective;
- performance goal;
- action map;
- course blueprint;
- storyboard or block plan;
- scenario/practice activity notes;
- assessment and action commitment;
- accessibility and localization requirements;
- safeguarding and civic-space constraints;
- AI authoring context;
- evaluation anchor;
- reviewer approval status.

Once approved, the Build Phase can open.

## E. Platform Design Implications

The Design Phase should appear in the Course Creator Portal as a structured workspace with clear controls, not as a blank document editor.

## 1. Read-only Analysis Reference Panel

The platform should keep the approved Analysis-to-Design Handover visible throughout the Design Phase. This prevents drift from the original capacity gap and baseline.

## 2. Action Map Workspace

The platform should provide a simple action mapping interface where creators define:

- capacity goal;
- required actions;
- practice activities;
- minimum information.

## 3. Course Blueprint Builder

The platform should provide structured fields for course architecture, learning pathway, learner constraints, assessment strategy, and evaluation linkage.

## 4. Storyboard and Block Planner

The platform should allow creators to plan the learner experience block by block before content is built.

## 5. Scenario and Practice Planner

The platform should provide a scenario planner when the diagnosis requires skill practice, judgment, motivation, safeguarding, advocacy, accountability, or decision-making.

## 6. Design Quality Gate

The platform should require review approval before Build opens. Build should remain locked until required design fields are completed and approved.

## 7. AI Context Package

The approved Design-to-Build Handover should become the context package for AI-assisted authoring. The AI assistant should use the approved capacity area, learner group, performance goal, tone, scenario context, safeguards, and block plan. It should not create unsupported objectives or unrelated content.

## 8. Persistent Traceability

Every course should remain traceable from:

Analysis evidence → capacity gap → indicator → baseline → root cause → intervention route → action map → blueprint → storyboard → Build → Review → Publish → Evaluate.

This traceability is the core quality logic of the DEC Learning Hub.

## F. Final Output of the Design Phase

The Design Phase produces an approved Design-to-Build Handover Record.

Minimum required fields:

| Field | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Course working title | Identifies the planned learning product |
| Linked Analysis Handover ID | Preserves traceability |
| Target learner group | Defines who the course is for |
| Capacity area | Links to DEC CSO capacity taxonomy |

| Baseline summary | Preserves the starting point |
| Performance goal | Defines what learners should do better |

| Storyboard / block plan | Defines the learner experience before Build |
| Assessment / action commitment | Defines evidence of learning or application |
| Safeguards and constraints | Captures civic-space, safeguarding, accessibility, bandwidth, and localization needs |
| Evaluation anchor | Carries the baseline and means of verification into Evaluate |
| Review status | Controls whether Build can open |

# Tools and Templates for the Design Phase

To keep the Design Phase lean and practical, it should use five tools only.

## Tool 2.1: Capacity Action Map Template

Purpose: Converts the approved capacity objective into observable learner actions, practice activities, and minimum required information.

Output: Approved action map.

1. Purpose of the Tool

The Capacity Action Map Template is the first tool in the Design Phase. It converts the approved Analysis-to-Design Handover Record into a clear map of what learners must actually do differently in their CSO work.

This tool prevents the course creator from starting with topics, lectures, slides, or general information. Instead, it requires the creator to begin with the approved capacity gap and work backwards from the desired CSO performance change.

The tool answers four questions:

1. What practical capacity goal must this course support?
2. What observable actions must learners perform to reach that goal?
3. How will learners practice those actions?
4. What minimum information do they need to complete the practice?

The output of this tool is an approved Capacity Action Map, which becomes the foundation for the Course Blueprint, Storyboard, Build Phase, and later Evaluation.

## Tool 2.2: Learning Design Document

Purpose: Turns the action map into a structured course plan, including learning pathway, module flow, assessment strategy, learner constraints, and evaluation linkage.

Output: Course blueprint / design contract.

1. Purpose of the Tool

The Learning Design Document is the second tool in the Design Phase. It converts the approved Capacity Action Map into a clear, structured course blueprint before any content is written or built.

This tool ensures that the course has a practical architecture: who it is for, what it is trying to change, what learners will practice, what format is most appropriate, how the course will be assessed, and what constraints must guide the Build Phase.

The Learning Design Document answers seven questions:

1. What is the course trying to help CSOs do better?
2. Who is the course specifically for?
3. What actions and practice activities will shape the course?

## Tool 2.4: Scenario and Practice Activity Planner

Purpose: Designs realistic CSO practice activities and scenario-based decision points where needed, especially for skill, judgment, advocacy, safeguarding, accountability, and motivation-related gaps.

Output: Scenario/practice logic plan.

1. Purpose of the Tool

The Scenario and Practice Activity Planner is used when the approved course design requires learners to practice a skill, make a decision, apply judgment, reflect on motivation, or prepare for a real CSO task.

This tool is especially important for capacity gaps related to:

- safeguarding;
- advocacy;
- accountability;
- MEAL;
- governance;
- donor compliance;
- resource mobilization;
- community engagement;
- data protection;
- partnership negotiation;
- leadership and decision-making.

## Tool 2.5: Design-to-Build Handover Record

Purpose: Consolidates the approved design into a locked handover that guides Build, constrains AI authoring, supports review, and preserves traceability to the Analysis Phase.

Output: Approved Design-to-Build Handover Record.

1. Purpose of the Tool

The Design-to-Build Handover Record is the final output of the Design Phase. It consolidates the approved outputs from the previous Design tools into one clear, structured, build-ready record.

This tool ensures that the Build Phase does not begin from a vague idea, a blank canvas, or a loose storyboard. It gives the course builder, reviewer, and AI-assisted authoring system a precise design package that explains:

- what course or learning product should be built;
- why it is needed;
- who it is for;
- what capacity gap it responds to;
- what learners must practice;
- what block sequence should be created;
- what scenario or practical activity logic must be followed;
- what assessment or action commitment is required;
- what accessibility, safeguarding, localization, and low-bandwidth constraints must be respected;
- what AI may and may not generate.

Once this handover is approved, the platform should lock the core design decisions and open the Build Phase.
