# Output 4 — A Guide for Triangulation, Validation, and Synthesis

## 1. Purpose of this guide

This guide explains how DEC, consortium leads, consultants, and analysis experts move from **raw field evidence** to a **final validated Analysis Record** that can be entered into the DEC Learning Hub platform.

The governing logic is simple: the platform should not store every raw interview note, FGD comment, or document extract. It should receive only the **validated, synthesized, platform-ready finding** through the final Analysis Data Entry Web Form. The Analysis Phase should work as a hybrid system: modular field tools first, then final validated data entry into a structured web form that feeds the database, native dashboard, and Course Creator Portal workflow.

The output of this guide is a clean, evidence-backed finding that can answer:

> What is the validated capacity gap, why is it happening, is it course-addressable, what safeguards apply, and is it ready for Design?

This sequence supports the required Analysis Phase work: comparing evidence from different tools, identifying patterns, handling conflicting evidence, validating findings, prioritizing gaps, deciding evidence confidence, classifying K/S/M/E root causes, separating course-addressable from non-course-addressable components, and preparing final findings for data entry.

## 3. Step 1 — Gather evidence from selected tools

The analysis team should first confirm which tools were used and what type of evidence each produced.

| Evidence source | Typical data produced | Strength | Limitation |
|---|---|---|---|
| CSO self-assessment | Perceived strengths, gaps, priorities, current practice | Good for ownership and self-reflection | Can be subjective or overly positive/negative |
| Key informant interviews | Detailed explanations, root causes, examples | Good for depth and context | May reflect individual bias or role perspective |
| Focus group discussions | Shared patterns, role differences, practical constraints | Good for team patterns and collective sensemaking | Some participants may dominate; sensitive views may be hidden |
| Document review | Existing reports, policies, logs, plans, assessments | Good for objective evidence of current practice | Documents may be outdated or not reflect real use |
| Validation workshop | Stakeholder confirmation, correction, prioritization | Good for legitimacy and consensus | Can become tokenistic if power dynamics are not managed |
| Prior assessments / donor feedback | External observations, historical findings | Useful for trend evidence | May not reflect current situation |
| Observation / work sample review | Actual outputs, templates, reports, records | Strong evidence of practice | May expose sensitive or confidential information |

**Minimum rule:** a gap should not normally be approved for Design from one weak evidence source alone. At least two credible sources should support the gap, or one strong source should be validated by relevant stakeholders.

## 4. Step 2 — Organize evidence by DEC capacity area

Before triangulation, evidence should be sorted under the nine DEC capacity areas. This keeps synthesis aligned with the Course Creator Portal taxonomy and prevents broad, vague course topics.

| DEC capacity area | Evidence examples to look for |
|---|---|
| Internal Governance and Leadership | board minutes, role clarity issues, conflict-of-interest records, decision-making gaps |
| Transparency and Accountability | feedback logs, complaints handling, public information, stakeholder communication |
| Strategic Planning and Organizational Sustainability | strategic plans, operational plans, risk logs, sustainability plans |
| Financial Management and Resource Mobilization | budgets, justifications, donor reports, funding pipeline, controls |
| Human Resources, Inclusion, and Safeguarding | HR policies, onboarding, safeguarding referral practice, inclusion gaps |
| Evidence-Based Advocacy and Civic Engagement | advocacy messages, policy briefs, constituency evidence, safe engagement practice |
| Monitoring, Evaluation, Accountability, and Learning | indicators, reports, outcome evidence, learning meeting notes, feedback use |
| Digital Skills and Data Use / IT Competencies | spreadsheet use, data cleaning, digital collaboration, information management |
| Networking, Partnerships, and Collective Action | MoUs, role matrices, coordination notes, coalition plans |

The taxonomy should function as structured product logic across Analysis, Design, Build, Publish, and Monitoring, including capacity area, sub-capacity, indicator/standard, evidence source, baseline/current practice, desired practice, K/S/M/E route, and course-fit decision.

## 5. Step 3 — Identify repeated patterns

A repeated pattern is stronger than a single complaint. The analysis team should look for patterns that appear across people, documents, roles, time periods, or CSOs.

### Pattern identification template

| Possible gap | Evidence source 1 | Evidence source 2 | Evidence source 3 | Pattern observed | Initial judgment |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Program staff struggle to write outcome evidence statements | Self-assessment: staff rate outcome reporting low | KII: MEAL lead says reports list activities only | Document review: donor reports show outputs but few outcomes | Same issue appears in perception, interview, and documents | Strong pattern |
| Board conflict-of-interest records are missing | Document review: no COI forms | KII: board member says issue is sensitive | FGD: staff unsure whether policy exists | Documentation and awareness gap; possible motivation issue | Needs careful K/S/M/E diagnosis |
| Feedback system not used | Self-assessment: complaint mechanism exists | FGD: field staff say communities do not trust it | Document review: few complaints recorded | Contradictory: system exists but not used | Need deeper diagnosis |

### Questions to ask

| Question | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Does the same issue appear in more than one source? | Increases credibility |
| Is the issue described similarly by different roles? | Shows shared understanding |
| Is the gap visible in actual work outputs? | Strengthens evidence |
| Is it recent? | Avoids acting on outdated evidence |
| Does the issue affect one CSO, one role, or a wider cohort? | Supports prioritization |
| Does the issue appear as a Knowledge/Skill problem or as a system/culture problem? | Supports K/S/M/E diagnosis |

## 6. Step 4 — Compare evidence using a triangulation matrix

Triangulation means comparing evidence sources to decide whether a gap is confirmed, partly confirmed, contradicted, or not supported.

### Triangulation matrix

| Gap candidate | Self-assessment evidence | KII evidence | FGD evidence | Document/work sample evidence | Validation evidence | Triangulation result |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Staff cannot prepare budget justifications linked to activities | Finance staff rate skill low | Program manager says budget lines are often weak | Program staff say they do not know donor cost logic | Previous proposal budget comments show weak justifications | CSO confirms this is a real gap | Confirmed |
| CSO lacks strategic planning capacity | Leadership says plan exists | Staff say plan is not used | FGD shows no quarterly planning rhythm | Strategic plan exists but no operational plan | Stakeholders confirm implementation gap, not planning document gap | Reframed |
| Staff need a report-writing course | Leadership says reports are weak | Staff say tablets fail offline | FGD confirms connectivity problems | Reports are late, but data system logs show syncing issues | Validation shows main barrier is system/internet | Not primarily course-addressable |

### Triangulation result options

| Result | Meaning | Next action |
|---|---|---|
| Confirmed | Multiple credible sources support the same gap | Proceed to validation, K/S/M/E, and prioritization |
| Partly confirmed | Evidence supports the gap but some details need clarification | Revise gap statement or collect limited additional evidence |
| Reframed | Evidence shows the original problem was wrongly described | Rewrite the gap before course-fit decision |
| Contradicted | Sources disagree in a way that affects interpretation | Investigate contradiction before approval |
| Not confirmed | Evidence does not support the proposed gap | Do not proceed to course design |
| Unsafe to verify further | More data collection could create risk | Use safe summary, specialist review, or stop |

## 7. Step 5 — Handle conflicting evidence

Contradictions are normal. They should not be hidden or averaged away. They should be used to improve diagnosis.

### Common types of conflict

| Conflict type | Example | How to handle it |
|---|---|---|
| Role-based conflict | Leaders say the policy works; field staff say it is not used | Compare with documents and work samples; validate with mixed-role discussion if safe |
| Perception vs evidence conflict | Staff say they are confident; work samples show repeated errors | Use work samples as strong evidence, but ask why confidence is high |
| Document vs practice conflict | CSO has a policy, but staff do not follow it | Diagnose whether gap is Knowledge, Skill, Motivation, or Environment |
| Old vs current evidence conflict | 2022 assessment shows a gap; 2026 staff say it improved | Use recent evidence unless older trend remains relevant |
| Sensitive topic conflict | Staff privately report safeguarding concerns; leadership denies issues | Protect confidentiality; avoid open validation that exposes staff |
| Course idea vs root cause conflict | Team wants training; evidence shows missing tools or system barriers | Route to non-course or blended support |

### Conflict resolution template

| Gap candidate | Contradiction | Possible explanation | Additional check needed? | Final decision |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Feedback mechanism not used | Management says it exists; field staff say communities do not use it | Mechanism exists on paper but is not trusted or accessible | Review feedback log and ask safe follow-up questions | Reframe as accountability practice gap with possible Motivation/Environment components |
| Poor donor reports | Leadership says staff lack writing skill; staff say data arrives late | Problem may be reporting system, not writing alone | Review reporting workflow and sample reports | Partly course-addressable if outcome writing skill exists; system issue routed separately |

**Rule:** when evidence conflicts, do not force a course-fit decision until the gap is reframed clearly.

## 8. Step 6 — Validate findings with CSOs and stakeholders

Validation should not be a ceremonial meeting where participants simply “agree” with a consultant’s finding. It should be a structured review where CSO actors can correct, refine, challenge, or prioritize the findings.

### Validation principles

| Principle | Practical meaning |
|---|---|
| Use safe summaries | Do not share raw quotes or sensitive examples that identify individuals |
| Validate with the right people | Include those who understand the actual practice, not only senior leadership |
| Avoid blame language | Frame gaps as capacity and system issues, not personal failure |
| Check usefulness | Ask whether the finding reflects a real work challenge |
| Check course-fit | Ask what can realistically improve through learning and what needs other support |
| Protect dissent | Allow anonymous input when topics are sensitive |
| Record changes | Document how validation changed the gap statement or route |

### Validation session structure

| Step | Activity | Output |
|---:|---|---|
| 1 | Present safe summary of evidence | Shared understanding |
| 2 | Confirm capacity area and sub-capacity | Correct taxonomy link |
| 3 | Review gap statement | Accepted or revised gap |
| 4 | Review baseline/current practice | Accepted or corrected baseline |
| 5 | Define desired practice | Realistic improvement statement |
| 6 | Review K/S/M/E diagnosis | Confirmed or revised root cause |
| 7 | Review course-fit | Course / blended / non-course route |
| 8 | Identify risks | Safeguards and visibility note |
| 9 | Prioritize | Priority score |
| 10 | Agree next step | Ready for data entry / needs more evidence / blocked |

### Validation status options

| Status | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Validated | Stakeholders confirm the finding is accurate and useful |
| Validated with revisions | Stakeholders confirm the issue but revise wording, scope, baseline, or route |
| Needs more evidence | Stakeholders cannot confirm without further checking |
| Not validated | Stakeholders reject or evidence does not support the finding |
| Validation limited | Validation was constrained by risk, availability, or sensitivity |
| Specialist validation required | Safeguarding, civic-space, legal, or technical review is needed |

## 9. Step 7 — Prioritize capacity gaps

Not every validated gap should become an immediate course pipeline priority. Prioritization helps DEC decide what to address first.

### Priority scoring matrix

Score each criterion from 1 to 5.

| Criterion | 1 = Low | 3 = Medium | 5 = High |
|---|---|---|---|
| Severity | Minor inconvenience | Affects quality or consistency | Blocks performance, compliance, safety, or trust |
| Frequency | Rare | Occasional | Common or repeated |
| Reach | One person or role | One team or several CSOs | Many CSOs, cohort-wide, or system-wide |
| Strategic relevance | Peripheral | Supports program priorities | Directly linked to DEC/EU CSO capacity objectives |
| Course feasibility | Weak learning fit | Partial learning fit | Strong Knowledge/Skill fit |
| Evidence strength | Anecdotal | Some triangulation | Strong multi-source evidence |
| Safeguards manageability | High unmanaged risk | Risk manageable with controls | Low risk or clearly manageable |
| Readiness for Design | Unclear | Needs refinement | Ready for Analysis-to-Design Handover |

### Priority category

| Priority category | Suggested rule | Recommended action |
|---|---|---|
| Priority A | High severity + strong evidence + clear K/S course fit + manageable risk | Prepare for final data entry and Design handover |
| Priority B | Important but partly course-addressable or needs complementary support | Prepare blended intervention route |
| Priority C | Valid but low urgency or weak course fit | Keep in gap bank; revisit later |
| Not ready | Evidence weak, conflict unresolved, or safeguard risk high | More diagnosis or specialist review |
| Non-course priority | High severity but mainly Motivation/Environment | Route to coaching, TA, leadership, systems, or enabling-environment action |

## 10. Step 8 — Decide evidence confidence level

Evidence confidence tells dashboard users and course creators how reliable the Analysis Record is.

### Evidence confidence scale

| Confidence level | Meaning | Minimum evidence expectation |
|---|---|---|
| High | The gap is strongly supported and validated | Multiple sources, recent evidence, work sample/document support, stakeholder validation |
| Medium | The gap is likely valid but some details remain uncertain | At least two sources or one strong source plus validation |
| Low | The gap is plausible but weakly supported | One main source, limited validation, or unclear evidence |
| Not confirmed | Evidence does not support the gap | Do not proceed |
| Unsafe to confirm fully | Further verification may create risk | Use restricted summary or specialist review |

### Confidence decision questions

| Question | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Are there at least two independent evidence sources? | Reduces bias |
| Is there evidence from actual work outputs or records? | Strengthens credibility |
| Was the finding validated by people close to the practice? | Supports usefulness |
| Is the evidence recent? | Avoids outdated course design |
| Are contradictions resolved or documented? | Prevents false certainty |
| Does the evidence avoid unsafe exposure? | Protects CSO trust |

## 11. Step 9 — Classify K/S/M/E root cause

After validation and confidence rating, classify the root cause.

### K/S/M/E classification guide

| Route | Diagnostic signal | Example | Course implication |
|---|---|---|---|
| Knowledge | Learners do not understand concepts, standards, steps, rules, or definitions | Staff do not understand the difference between outputs and outcomes | Course-addressable |
| Skill | Learners understand the idea but cannot perform the task, use the tool, make the decision, or produce the output | Staff know what an outcome is but cannot write an evidence statement | Course-addressable |
| Motivation | Learners know what to do but do not act because of confidence, incentives, leadership signals, ownership, culture, fear, or perceived value | Staff can collect feedback but do not believe management will use it | Not course-addressable alone |
| Environment | Learners are blocked by tools, systems, time, staffing, funding, policies, infrastructure, civic-space, or safety conditions | Staff cannot submit digital reports because devices/internet are unavailable | Not course-addressable alone |
| Mixed | Several causes exist together | Staff lack feedback categorization skill, and the CSO lacks an escalation mechanism | Proceed only for separable K/S component |
| Unclear | Evidence is insufficient or contradictory | Different sources point to different causes | Needs further diagnosis |

The DEC implementation logic requires Phase 1 to prioritize Knowledge and Skill gaps and to block Motivation or Environment gaps from driving course production unless a separable Knowledge/Skill component is explicitly recorded.

### K/S/M/E diagnostic worksheet

| Validated gap | Knowledge component | Skill component | Motivation component | Environment component | Final route | Separable K/S component? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Staff cannot write outcome evidence statements | Confuse output/outcome | Cannot synthesize evidence | Reporting seen as donor compliance | No learning meeting structure | Mixed | Yes: outcome evidence writing |
| Feedback mechanism not used | Staff know categories | Can use forms | Communities do not trust mechanism | No safe escalation process | Environment/Motivation | No, not yet |
| Budget justifications are weak | Do not know allowable cost logic | Cannot link budget to activity | Program/finance teams do not coordinate | No shared template | Mixed | Yes: budget justification skill |

## 12. Step 10 — Separate course-addressable and non-course components

A single gap may contain several components. The Analysis Record must clearly separate what a course can address from what needs other support.

### Separation template

| Validated gap | Course-addressable K/S component | Non-course component | Complementary support needed | Design implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Program officers cannot produce outcome evidence statements | Teach output/outcome distinction; practice writing evidence statements | Management does not hold learning reviews | Leadership coaching or learning meeting routine | Course can proceed for evidence writing only |
| Staff do not use community feedback data | Teach feedback categorization if needed | Communities do not trust the mechanism; no escalation policy | Accountability system strengthening | Course should not proceed unless system component is addressed |
| Board members do not document conflicts of interest | Explain conflict-of-interest concept and record format | Board culture discourages challenging powerful members | Governance mentoring and leadership engagement | Course can address procedure knowledge, but not culture alone |
| Advocacy staff produce risky public messages | Teach safe message drafting and anonymization | Civic-space risk and public authority sensitivity | Civic-space specialist review and risk protocol | Course may proceed only with safeguards and safe scenarios |

### Course-fit decision rules

| If the diagnosis is... | Then... |
|---|---|
| Knowledge only | Course can proceed if evidence and safeguards are sufficient |
| Skill only | Course can proceed if practice tasks and assessment can be designed |
| Knowledge + Skill | Strong course candidate |
| Motivation only | Block course; route to leadership, coaching, peer learning, incentives, or culture support |
| Environment only | Block course; route to systems, tools, resources, policies, TA, or enabling-environment action |
| Mixed with separable K/S | Proceed only for K/S component; record complementary support |
| Mixed without separable K/S | Do not proceed to course design |
| Unclear | More diagnosis required |

## 13. Step 11 — Apply safeguards and no-harm review

Before final data entry, every validated finding should be screened for safety.

### Safeguards review table

| Risk area | Questions | Data-entry implication |
|---|---|---|
| Safeguarding/protection | Does the evidence involve children, GBV, protection cases, referrals, or vulnerable people? | Do not enter raw cases; use anonymized/simulated summaries |
| Civic-space sensitivity | Could the finding expose advocacy actors, CSOs, communities, or rights-holders? | Restrict visibility; require specialist review |
| Personal data | Are names, contacts, photos, case files, or identifiable stories included? | Remove or anonymize before platform entry |
| Organizational vulnerability | Could dashboard display shame, rank, or expose a CSO weakness? | Use safe aggregation and role-sensitive visibility |
| Donor sensitivity | Could the finding be misused as compliance surveillance? | Restrict donor-facing visibility; use safe summaries only |
| Practical proof risk | Would later proof require unsafe uploads? | Define safe proof alternatives |
| AI prompt safety | Could AI receive sensitive raw data? | Exclude sensitive data from AI context |

The native dashboard and platform integration must be safe by design. Approved Analysis Records should feed the workflow directly, while core Analysis fields become read-only after approval or locking; the native dashboard must operate inside the portal rather than as an external BI layer.

## 16. Good and weak synthesis examples

### Example A — Strong synthesis

| Element | Good entry |
|---|---|
| Capacity area | Monitoring, Evaluation, Accountability, and Learning |
| Gap | Program officers cannot write short outcome evidence statements for donor and internal learning reports. |
| Baseline | Current reports mostly list activities, participant numbers, and completed events, with limited explanation of what changed for participants or communities. |
| Desired practice | Program officers can write a short outcome evidence statement that links one observed change to one indicator and one evidence source, while noting confidence and limitation. |
| Evidence | Self-assessment, MEAL lead interview, three sampled reports, validation workshop. |
| K/S/M/E | Mixed: Knowledge gap on output vs outcome; Skill gap in writing evidence statements; Motivation issue because reports are seen mainly as donor compliance. |
| Separable K/S component | Yes: outcome evidence writing skill. |
| Course-fit | Partly course-addressable. Course can address output/outcome distinction and evidence statement writing. Motivation issue should be addressed through management learning review practice. |
| Safeguards | Use anonymized project examples; avoid beneficiary-identifiable stories. |
| Evaluation anchor | Final test scenario and optional anonymized outcome evidence worksheet. |

### Example B — Weak synthesis

| Element | Weak entry | Why weak |
|---|---|---|
| Gap | CSOs need MEAL training. | Too broad; not a capacity gap statement. |
| Baseline | Weak MEAL. | No concrete current practice. |
| Desired practice | Strong MEAL system. | Too vague and unrealistic for one course. |
| Evidence | Staff said so. | No triangulation. |
| K/S/M/E | Knowledge. | Unsupported diagnosis. |
| Course-fit | Course needed. | Assumes training is the solution. |
| Safeguards | None. | No review of data sensitivity. |

### Example C — Reframed after triangulation

| Element | Initial assumption | Reframed finding |
|---|---|---|
| Problem | Staff need report-writing training. | Staff cannot submit timely reports because offline data collection tools fail and reporting templates are inconsistent. |
| K/S/M/E | Assumed Skill | Mixed, mainly Environment with possible Skill component |
| Course-fit | Full report-writing course | Do not proceed with broad course. Consider short template-use training only after reporting template/system issue is addressed. |

## 17. Handoff from synthesis to final Analysis Data Entry Form

The synthesis team should hand over only finalized, safe, validated entries to the data-entry user.

| Handoff item | Required? | Purpose |

| Capacity area and sub-capacity | Yes | Taxonomy and dashboard grouping |
| Baseline/current practice | Yes | Evaluation and design anchor |

| Validation status | Yes | Approval readiness |
| Evidence confidence level | Yes | Decision quality |
| K/S/M/E route | Yes | Course-fit gate |
| Separable K/S component | Conditional | Required for Mixed, Motivation, or Environment cases |
| Course-fit decision | Yes | Design unlock/block logic |
| Complementary support route | Conditional | Required for partly course-addressable or non-course cases |

The final Analysis Data Entry Web Form should capture the final validated, synthesized, platform-ready Analysis Record, with required, conditional, and optional fields. It should feed the database, the native Analysis Dashboard, and the Course Creator Portal's Design prefill/read-only logic.
