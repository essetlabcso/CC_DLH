## 4. Analysis / Diagnosis sample

### Capacity gap statement

CSO reports describe activities and attendance but do not clearly explain what changed, for whom, and what evidence supports the change.

### Evidence source

- review of project reports;
- donor feedback;
- MEAL coaching observations;
- CSO self-assessment;
- sample activity reports.

### Evidence type

```text
Document review
Training/coaching observation
Self-assessment
Program staff feedback
```

### Source confidence

```text
Medium to high
```

### Current baseline / current practice

Project and MEAL staff usually report:

- number of trainings delivered;
- number of participants reached;
- activities completed;
- photos or attendance sheets;
- general statements such as “awareness increased.”

They often do not explain:

- what changed;
- who changed;
- how the change was observed;
- what evidence supports the claim;
- what the CSO learned or adapted.

### Desired practice

Project and MEAL staff can write a concise outcome evidence statement that explains:

- what changed;
- for whom;
- compared to what baseline/current practice;
- what evidence supports the change;
- what the CSO learned or will adapt.

### Root cause route

```text
Skill + Knowledge
```

### Course-fit decision

```text
Course-addressable with complementary template support
```

### Separable K/S component

Learners need to understand the difference between activity reporting and outcome evidence, then practice writing a simple evidence-based outcome statement.

### Motivation/Environment notes

Some CSOs may also need management routines, templates, and time for reflection meetings. These are complementary support needs and should not replace the course’s Knowledge/Skill focus.

### Safeguards / no-harm note

Learners should not include sensitive personal data, beneficiary names, protection case details, or politically sensitive information in outcome evidence examples. They should use anonymized or aggregated evidence.

### Data sensitivity note

Moderate. Outcome evidence may include community or participant information. The course must teach anonymization, consent, and safe summary practices.

### Evaluation anchor

Improvement can be assessed through:

- final test on outcome evidence concepts and judgment;
- practical proof: submitted outcome evidence worksheet;
- monitoring of certificate completion;
- verified achievement count for accepted practical proof.

## 7. Learning Design sample

### Learning goal

By the end of the course, participants can draft a safe, concise, evidence-based outcome statement from a project activity example.

### Learning objectives

Participants will be able to:

1. Explain the difference between activities, outputs, outcomes, and evidence.
2. Identify weak outcome claims in CSO reports.
3. Select safe and relevant evidence to support an outcome claim.
4. Draft an outcome evidence statement using a simple template.
5. Apply anonymization and data safety principles when writing outcome evidence.

### Lesson pathway

```text
Lesson 1 — Why activity reporting is not enough
Lesson 2 — What makes a strong outcome evidence statement
Lesson 3 — Practice selecting safe evidence
Lesson 4 — Drafting your own outcome evidence statement
Lesson 5 — Final test and optional practical proof
```

### Assessment approach

- knowledge checks after key concepts;
- scenario decisions on safe evidence;
- final test with 80% certificate threshold;
- optional practical proof through completed worksheet.

### Accessibility and localization

- plain language;
- short text blocks;
- downloadable worksheet;
- low-bandwidth alternative to video;
- examples from local CSO reporting practice;
- avoid donor jargon where possible.

### Safeguarding/data safety

- do not request real beneficiary names;
- use fictionalized examples;
- teach redaction/anonymization;
- proof instructions must require safe evidence.

## 10. Final Test sample

### Fixed rule

```text
Pass/certificate threshold:
80%
```

### Question 1

```text
Type:
Multiple choice

Prompt:
Which statement is the strongest outcome evidence statement?

Options:
A. We trained 25 participants on outcome reporting.
B. Participants attended the full training and received handouts.
C. After the training, 18 of 25 participants correctly revised a weak outcome statement using the provided checklist.
D. The training was successful and participants were happy.

Correct answer:
C

Feedback:
C is strongest because it describes what changed, for whom, and what evidence supports the change.
```

### Question 2

```text
Type:
Scenario-based multiple choice

Prompt:
A project officer wants to include the full name and phone number of a community member in an outcome evidence example. What should they do?

Options:
A. Include the details because names make evidence stronger.
B. Remove or anonymize personal details and summarize the evidence safely.
C. Add a photo instead of the phone number.
D. Ask the donor to decide later.

Correct answer:
B

Feedback:
Outcome evidence should be useful but safe. Personal and sensitive details should be removed unless there is a clear, consented, and safe reason.
```

### Question 3

```text
Type:
True/false

Prompt:
A course certificate proves that the learner has passed the final test, but it does not automatically prove that the CSO has changed its organizational practice.

Correct answer:
True

Feedback:
The certificate confirms the learner passed the course test. Practical proof and verified achievement are separate evidence of application.
```
